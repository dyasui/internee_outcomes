* Introduction
My research setting centers around the large-scale forced evacuation and incarceration of over one hundred thousand Japanese Americans away from the US West Coast during World War Two.


Japanese Internment can be used as a natural experiment to test the the impacts of forced migration on the affected migrants themselves.
My research analyses how interned Japanese Americans labor market outcomes, choice of occupation, and later migration after release from internment camps were affected relative to non-interned Japanese Americans and other similar immigrant groups.
This research approaches this question with newly available linked panel data to follow the individual outcomes of thousands of internees between the 1940 and 1950 decennial census.


** Research Question
What was the effect of internment on wage growth?
** Motivation
Different studies seem to disagree on the sign of the effect.
How to square the differences?
- Who was hurt more by internment? Who could adapt?
- Mechanisms: migration, rural vs. urban, occupational change?

Why it matters: different implications for other related questions.
- Adaptation story is an opptimistic take on forced migration; people are resilient
- But inequality of opportunity also matters; if only realtively men benefitted, only young benefitted, etc. matters for targetted policy
** Contributions
1. Synthesize different findings around internment outcomes
2. Use more data to look at heterogeneous effects
3. Model migration decision after release from camp to identify tradeoffs of forced relocation
